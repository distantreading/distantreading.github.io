WG1 : the story so far

What is Work Group 1 for?

The aim of this COST Action is to "develop the resources and methods necessary to change the way European literary history is written." (MOU, p.2)

An important part of that goal is to create a big collection of literature from 1850-1920 which can be used to benchmark different computational distant-reading methods for corpus annotation and analysis: we call this the European Literary Text Collection (ELTeC)

WG1 was charged with ...

  • coordination, construction, and publication of the ELTeC
  • providing advice to partners concerning the structuring of ELTeC subcollections
  • in particular as regards encoding, linguistic annotation and metadata

Sampling

Following a suggestion from WG3, we divided the proposals document into two papers,

  • http://distantreading.github.io/canonicity_proposal.html discusses the theory behind this process

Recommendations (in summary):

  • aim to represent variety not population, for each of four criteria:
  • size of work
  • date of creation
  • sex of author
  • endurance ("canonicity") of work

Encoding scheme

Following discussion in Prague, we defined three ‘levels’ of encoding; TEI schemas exist for eltec-0 and eltec-1. Level 2 proposals will be determined by the outcomes of this meeting!

The schemas are defined using the TEI ODD system which enables us to generate comprehensive documentation (see e.g. https://distantreading.github.io/Schema/eltec-0.html) : work is ongoing on customizing this.

We produced a preliminary set of training materials for a two day Workshop held in Wuerzburg in April. A dozen participants were taken through the process of converting a PDF file to a fully marked up ELTEc text, using Abbyy fine reader and oXygen.

ELTeC requirements for its schema

  • There should be a guaranteed minimum level of encoding for all texts in the ELTeC.
  • The encoding should reflect the analytic needs of ‘distant reading’, rather than those of the digital editor.
  • At the same time, the encoding should not misrepresent the text as originally created and received.
  • The encoding must be easy to apply in a consistent way, starting from both basic OCR and pre-existing digital versions.

How do we square this circle?

A multi-level schema

TEI permits what is called ODD chaining, in which one schema is derived from another. We use this technique to define three ‘levels’ of ELTeC schema,

  • level zero : the basic minimum guaranteed to be available for every ELTeC text, requiring minimal human intervention for its creation;
  • level one : some additional distinctions are made (for example amongst passages which are typographically salient) for which human judgment may be needed;
  • level two : linguistic and semantic annotation is added,at the level of individual tokens and segments, typically by means of an automatic process.

Still subject to discussion in WG1 and WG2 !

Changing levels

  • The different ‘levels’ of the ELTeC schema are not really levels in the sense that a text will progress majestically from 0 to 2.
  • It is easy to go from 1 or 2 to 0: a stylesheet simply has to remove some tagging automatically
  • It is also easy (assuming the right software environment) to go from from 0 or 1 to 2: linguistic annotations can be added automatically
  • It is not easy to go from 0 or 2 to 1: human judgment may be required

Why the three levels?

  • texts may come from many sources, some of which already have extensive tagging
  • some (though by no means all) of that tagging is useful for distant reading purposes and we would like to be able to retain it rather than have the expense of adding it. For example :
    • distinguishing verse and prose
    • distinguishing highlighted phrases and their functions (e.g. emphasis, foreign, titles)
    • identifying footnotes
    • (maybe) distinguishing direct speech from narrative
  • texts which don't make such distinctions are still useful (of course!); the core functions of distant reading (as so far defined) don't require these distinctions, though they may benefit from them.
  • identifying a text as level 1 or as level 0 guarantees a specific level of encoding

Workflow

We first tested our proposals using a sample of texts contributed by volunteers in a variety of formats and languages, which were all converted to ELTeC (with varying degrees of difficulty). The results are still available as the ELTeC Sampler (12 texts)

We defined a procedure for individual researchers to upload their texts to identically structured repositories on Github, one per language: https://github.com/distantreading/WG1/wiki/Versioning-Guidelines-for-ELTeC

The process of validating texts and uploading them has begun! See https://github.com/COST-ELTeC for the current state of affairs.`

    • Punctuation distinctions: hyphen vs emdash, curly vs straight quotes
